## 8. BRAND STRATEGY & POSITIONING

### Brand Positioning Frameworks

**Perceptual Map:**
- Two-axis grid plotting your brand and competitors on meaningful dimensions
- Axes must have clear, contrasting ends (e.g., "affordable vs premium" and "traditional vs modern")
- Reveals white space opportunities — where NO competitor plays
- Update regularly as market shifts

**Brand Diamond:**
- Four facets: Attributes (what you have), Benefits (what you give), Values (what you believe), Personality (how you behave)
- Each facet must support the others — internal contradiction = brand confusion

**Brand Key:**
- Root Strength → Competitive Environment → Target → Insight → Benefits → Values → Reasons to Believe → Discriminator → Brand Essence
- Forces linear, logical thinking about what makes the brand truly different

### Jung's 12 Brand Archetypes

Codified for branding by Margaret Mark & Carol S. Pearson in "The Hero and the Outlaw" (2001). Based on Carl Jung's theory of universal character patterns in the collective unconscious.

| Archetype | Desire | Strategy | Brand Examples |
|-----------|--------|----------|----------------|
| **Innocent** | Safety | Be good, do right | Dove, Coca-Cola, Aveeno |
| **Explorer** | Freedom | Seek adventure, discover | Patagonia, Jeep, The North Face |
| **Sage** | Knowledge | Seek truth, understand | Google, TED, BBC |
| **Hero** | Mastery | Act courageously | Nike, FedEx, BMW |
| **Outlaw** | Liberation | Break the rules | Harley-Davidson, Virgin, Diesel |
| **Magician** | Power | Make dreams real | Apple, Disney, Tesla |
| **Regular Guy** | Belonging | Connect authentically | IKEA, Target, Budweiser |
| **Lover** | Intimacy | Attract and be attracted | Chanel, Victoria's Secret, Godiva |
| **Jester** | Enjoyment | Have fun, be playful | Old Spice, M&M's, Ben & Jerry's |
| **Caregiver** | Service | Help and protect others | Johnson & Johnson, UNICEF, Volvo |
| **Creator** | Innovation | Create something new | Adobe, Lego, Crayola |
| **Ruler** | Control | Lead and organize | Mercedes-Benz, Rolex, Microsoft |

**Usage rules:**
- Choose ONE primary archetype (trying to be everything = being nothing)
- Can have a secondary archetype for texture (e.g., Hero primary + Explorer secondary)
- Archetype informs voice, visual style, messaging, and customer experience
- Align personality, design cues, and rituals across all touchpoints

### Brand Naming Principles

**Linguistic considerations:**
- Pronunciation across target languages
- Negative meanings in other languages (test exhaustively)
- Mouth-feel: how it sounds when spoken aloud
- Length: 1-3 syllables ideal for memorability

**Types of brand names:**
- **Descriptive**: PayPal, YouTube (easy to understand, hard to own)
- **Invented**: Kodak, Xerox, Google (unique, ownable, requires investment to build meaning)
- **Metaphorical**: Amazon, Apple, Nike (borrowed associations from existing concepts)
- **Acronymic**: IBM, BMW, KFC (efficient but can lack personality)
- **Founder**: Chanel, Ford, Dell (heritage and credibility, but limits brand evolution)

**Practical checklist:**
- Domain availability (.com still matters)
- Social handle availability across platforms
- Trademark clearance in target markets
- No negative connotations in target languages
- Easy to spell, easy to say, easy to remember
- Works as a verb? ("Google it" = ultimate brand power)

### Brand Narrative Frameworks

**The Origin Story**: Why the founder started. What problem they saw. What they believed differently.
**The Enemy Story**: What the brand stands AGAINST (Supreme vs mass production; Apple vs "Big Brother" IBM)
**The Customer Story**: The transformation the customer undergoes (before → after)
**The Mission Story**: The change the brand seeks to make in the world (Patagonia saving the planet)
