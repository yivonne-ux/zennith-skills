## 1. THE 2026 AESTHETIC

### The Big Shift: "Refined Authenticity"

2026 food photography sits at the intersection of two forces:

**MAXIMALISM is back** — Pinterest's 2026 trend predictions show Millennials and Gen Z embracing more colorful palettes, moving away from the all-beige/neutral aesthetics of 2023-2024. More color. More texture. More vibrancy.

**BUT with honest restraint** — Food styling in 2026 is about restraint and realism. Stylists focus less on manipulation and more on enhancement, allowing ingredients to look like their best, most natural selves. Content that feels slightly imperfect performs better because it feels natural.

### The Winning Formula

```
WARM + NATURAL + SLIGHTLY IMPERFECT + VIBRANT COLOR + INTENTIONAL COMPOSITION
```

The 2026 aesthetic is NOT:
- Overly filtered/processed (the Instagram 2018 look is dead)
- Perfectly symmetrical (too artificial)
- Cool/blue-toned (makes food unappetizing)
- Busy/cluttered (loses focus on the dish)
- Flat-lit (looks like cafeteria food)

The 2026 aesthetic IS:
- Warm-toned (5500-6000K color temperature, slightly golden)
- Soft directional light with embraced shadows (not eliminated)
- "Messy chic" — artful imperfection (a crumb here, a drizzle there)
- Real food, real portions, honest presentation
- Rich in texture — you can almost feel the food through the screen
- Bold color pops against neutral/warm backgrounds

### Key Insight for GrabFood Enhancement

Food photography in 2026 must feel REAL, DESIRABLE, and THOUGHTFULLY MADE. The era of over-processed, heavily filtered food shots is dead. Slightly imperfect = more appetizing. The goal is to make food look like its BEST natural self, not a fake version of itself.

## 2. SURFACES & BACKGROUNDS

### Trending Surface Materials (Ranked by Pinterest Saves)

#### TIER 1: Most Saved / Most Premium
1. **Raw/Reclaimed Wood** — Warm grain patterns create natural leading lines that guide the eye. Dark smooth wood conveys premium/fine dining. Light wood conveys artisanal/rustic.
2. **Marble (White/Grey Veined)** — Cool, sleek, modern elegance. Works especially well with desserts and colorful dishes. The veining adds visual interest without competing.
3. **Textured Concrete/Stone** — Dark charcoal concrete for moody premium shots. Light grey for modern clean looks. Raw stone for organic/natural brands.

#### TIER 2: Strong Performers
4. **Ceramic/Handmade Tiles** — Trending hard in 2026. Zellige tiles, handmade terracotta, glazed ceramic. Adds artisanal character.
5. **Linen/Natural Fabric** — Rumpled linen in cream, oatmeal, or sage. Adds softness and warmth. Never perfectly ironed — the wrinkles are the aesthetic.
6. **Slate/Dark Stone** — Premium for grills, sizzlers, steaks, rich curries. Dark backgrounds make colors pop intensely.

#### TIER 3: Specific Use Cases
7. **Clean White** — The gold standard for delivery app thumbnails (max contrast, zero distraction). Works with every food color. Essential for ecommerce product shots.
8. **Bold Brand Color** — Solid color matched to brand identity. Stops the scroll in a sea of white. Best for featured items and promotions.

### Key Surface Pairing Rules

| Food Type | Best Surface | Why |
|-----------|-------------|-----|
| Asian noodles/rice | Dark wood, slate | Contrast with pale starches |
| Colorful curries | White/cream, light marble | Let the color be the hero |
| Grilled meats | Dark concrete, charcoal | Premium, moody, masculine |
| Desserts/pastries | Marble, light wood | Clean, elegant, aspirational |
| Salads/fresh | Raw wood, linen | Organic, natural, healthy |
| Soups/bowls | Dark slate, textured concrete | Warmth contrast against cool stone |

### The 2026 Surface Trend

**Texture contrast is king.** The most striking trend is deliberately blending contrasting textures: smooth ceramic on rough wood, polished marble with raw linen, glazed tiles on textured concrete. The interplay creates visual depth that flat surfaces cannot.

## 3. PLATING & STYLING

### Dominant Plating Styles (2026)

#### 1. "Messy Chic" / Authentic Imperfection (MOST VIRAL)
- Crumbs scattered naturally around a pastry
- Slight sauce drizzle that's not perfectly placed
- A herb sprig fallen "haphazardly"
- Real portions, not food-styled miniatures
- Chopsticks resting casually, not posed perfectly
- **Why it works**: Feels real. Makes viewers think "I could be eating this right now."

#### 2. Geometric / Structured (RISING)
- Clean lines, structured shapes
- Precision-cut ingredients arranged deliberately
- Jewel-toned gelatin cubes, layered sandwiches, architectural plating
- Negative space as a design element
- **Why it works**: Instagram-ready. Feels designed and premium without being fake.

#### 3. Immersive "Foodscapes" (TRENDING)
- Multiple dishes creating a scene/tablescape
- Seasonal produce scattered around the main dish
- Ingredients as decorative elements (whole spices, fresh herbs, citrus)
- The "scene" tells a story — a meal in progress, a gathering
- **Why it works**: Aspirational lifestyle. Pinterest users save these as "mood" inspiration.

#### 4. Single Hero Plate (CLASSIC/ECOMMERCE)
- One dish, shot from above or 45 degrees
- Maximum negative space
- Minimal props — maybe one garnish, one sauce element
- Clean background
- **Why it works**: Maximum clarity for menus and delivery apps.

### Plating Rules That Make Food Look Premium

1. **Odd numbers** — 3 elements, 5 garnish pieces, never 2 or 4
2. **Height variation** — Stack, layer, lean elements against each other
3. **Sauce as art** — Swooshes, dots, drizzles (never a pool)
4. **Garnish with purpose** — Fresh herbs that relate to the dish, not random parsley
5. **Rim discipline** — Keep food away from plate edges (the "breathing room" effect)
6. **Color contrast on plate** — Dark plate + bright food, or white plate + rich-colored food

## 5. LIGHTING

### The Definitive Lighting Hierarchy for Food Photography

#### BEST: Side-Backlight (9-11 o'clock position)
- Light positioned slightly behind and to the side of the food
- Creates a natural "glow" on the top edges of the food (specular highlights)
- Shows depth, texture, and dimension
- Especially magical for: glazed foods, sauces, oils, translucent ingredients, beverages
- Creates natural shadows that add drama without being harsh
- **This is the "Pinterest look" — the glowing, warm, dimensional food shot**

#### GREAT: Side Light (3 o'clock or 9 o'clock position)
- Most versatile setup, works for nearly everything
- Emphasizes texture: crispy fried foods, grilled meats, textured toppings
- Creates depth and dimension
- Easy to control with a single reflector on the opposite side
- **The workhorse of commercial food photography**

#### GOOD: Backlight (12 o'clock, behind the food)
- Creates a halo/glow effect around edges
- Makes translucent foods glow (thin sauces, beverages, fruits)
- Foods with oil/glaze become luminous
- Requires a fill reflector in front to avoid dark shadows
- **The "hero shot" light — dramatic but harder to control**

#### AVOID: Direct Overhead
- Creates flat, shadowless images
- Food looks like cafeteria/institutional food
- No depth, no texture, no dimension
- **This is what most hawker stall / cheap food photos use — the ceiling light**

#### NEVER: Direct Flash (On-Camera Flash)
- Kills all shadows and texture
- Creates harsh highlights and blown-out spots
- Makes food look wet/greasy in a bad way
- **The hallmark of amateur food photography**

### Lighting Quality Rules

1. **ALWAYS DIFFUSE** — Never use harsh/direct light on food. Softboxes, diffusion panels, even parchment paper as a diffuser.
2. **EMBRACE SHADOWS** — 2026 trend is shadows as a design element, not something to eliminate. Shadows add realism and atmosphere.
3. **WARM IT UP** — Use gold reflectors or warm color temperature. Cool light makes food look unappetizing.
4. **SINGLE SOURCE** — One main light + one reflector (fill). Multiple lights create confusing shadows.

### For GrabFood Enhancement: What to Fix
Most hawker/restaurant phone photos suffer from:
- Overhead fluorescent lighting (flat, green-cast, unflattering)
- Mixed color temperatures (warm bulbs + cool daylight = muddy colors)
- No directional light (no shadows = no dimension)
- Flash burnout (dead white highlights, no texture)

**Enhancement target**: Simulate soft side-backlight with warm color temperature. Add subtle directional shadows. Remove green/cool color casts. Bring back texture through contrast and clarity.

## 6. PROPS & STYLING ELEMENTS

### Props That Make Photos Go Viral (2026)

#### MUST-HAVE Props
1. **Fresh herbs** — Scattered basil, cilantro, mint, rosemary. Not placed perfectly — scattered naturally. The #1 "make it look professional" hack.
2. **Sauce drips & drizzles** — Honey, soy sauce, chili oil, melted cheese. Caught mid-pour or freshly drizzled. Adds dynamism.
3. **Human hands** — A hand reaching for bread, holding chopsticks, pouring sauce. Adds story and emotion. Makes the viewer feel present.
4. **Linen napkins/cloth** — Rumpled, natural, imperfect. Cream, oatmeal, sage, or dusty rose colors. Adds texture and warmth.
5. **Raw ingredients** — Scatter the ingredients of the dish around it: whole spices, sliced citrus, loose noodles, fresh chili.

#### ELEVATED Props
6. **Chopsticks/Utensils** — Resting casually on the bowl edge or placed diagonally. Never perfectly parallel.
7. **Small bowls of condiments** — Chili sauce, soy sauce, sambal. In small ceramic dishes (never the plastic squeeze bottles).
8. **Beverages** — A glass of tea, iced drink, or water adds "meal scene" context.
9. **Parchment/baking paper** — Adds texture under baked goods, wrapped around sandwiches.
10. **Wooden boards/platters** — For presenting flatbreads, grilled items, sharing plates.

#### TRENDING in 2026
11. **Imperfect ceramics** — Handmade, unglazed pottery. Wabi-sabi aesthetic. Each piece unique.
12. **Woven baskets** — Natural materials trending hard. Bamboo steamers, rattan trays.
13. **Cast iron** — Skillet still in the frame, food served "from the pan." Suggests freshness.
14. **Edible flowers** — Small, deliberate placement. Not 2016-style "cover everything in flowers."

### Prop Rules for Premium Look

| DO | DON'T |
|----|-------|
| Use matte/textured ceramics | Use shiny white plastic plates |
| Scatter 2-3 herbs naturally | Dump a pile of garnish |
| Show real cutlery (metal) | Show plastic forks/spoons |
| Use cloth napkins | Use paper napkins in frame |
| Include 1-2 contextual items | Clutter the frame with props |
| Leave negative space | Fill every inch |
| Use odd numbers of elements | Use even/symmetrical arrangements |

## 7. CAMERA ANGLES

### Which Angle Converts Best?

#### 45-Degree Angle — THE KING FOR DELIVERY APPS
- **Most versatile, most commercial, most natural**
- Mimics how you'd see the food sitting at a table
- Shows the front AND surface of the dish, plus the sides
- Creates depth and makes food look 3D
- **This is the angle for GrabFood menu photos**
- Best for: Bowls, burgers, rice dishes, layered items, anything with HEIGHT

#### Overhead/Flat Lay — BEST FOR INSTAGRAM, FLAT FOODS
- Shows all elements from above equally
- Works for food that is flat or has detail on the top surface
- Best for: Pizza, salads in bowls, nasi lemak spread, poke bowls, flatbreads
- Also great for "foodscapes" — multiple dishes in a scene
- NOT ideal for tall items (burgers, drinks, stacked food)

#### Straight-On (Eye Level) — FOR DRAMATIC/HERO SHOTS
- Maximum drama, shows height and layers
- Best for: Burgers, stacked pancakes, tall drinks, layer cakes
- Creates the "wow" factor
- Less common for delivery apps but great for social media

### For GrabFood Enhancement
**Default to 45-degree framing.** If the original photo is taken from overhead and the dish has height, the enhancement should simulate a slight tilt toward 45 degrees through composition adjustments. If the food is flat (rice plates, spread dishes), overhead works fine.

## 8. WHAT MAKES FOOD PHOTOS LOOK "EXPENSIVE" VS "CHEAP"

### The 12 Factors

| # | EXPENSIVE Look | CHEAP Look |
|---|---------------|------------|
| 1 | **Soft directional light** with natural shadows | Flat overhead fluorescent or harsh flash |
| 2 | **Warm color temperature** (golden, inviting) | Cool/green cast (clinical, institutional) |
| 3 | **Shallow depth of field** (blurred background) | Everything in focus equally (phone camera default) |
| 4 | **Intentional composition** with negative space | Random framing, dish cut off at edges |
| 5 | **Matte/textured ceramics** or quality plates | Styrofoam, plastic containers, paper plates |
| 6 | **Clean surfaces** with subtle texture | Cluttered table, visible mess, stained surfaces |
| 7 | **Fresh garnish** (herbs, microgreens, seeds) | No garnish or wilted/old garnish |
| 8 | **Color-corrected** warm tones | Unedited, blue/grey/muddy tones |
| 9 | **Height and dimension** in plating | Flat pile of food on plate |
| 10 | **Sauce as design** (swoosh, drizzle, dots) | Sauce pooled or mixed in |
| 11 | **Single hero focus** with supporting elements | Too many dishes competing for attention |
| 12 | **Clean rim/edge** on serving vessel | Food spilled over edges, messy presentation |

### The Three Things That INSTANTLY Elevate Any Food Photo

1. **WARM LIGHT DIRECTION** — Even a phone photo looks 10x better when taken near a window with natural side light vs under ceiling fluorescents.
2. **A PROPER PLATE** — Same nasi lemak on a matte ceramic plate vs a styrofoam box is a completely different photo.
3. **ONE FRESH HERB GARNISH** — A few coriander leaves, a sprig of basil, sliced spring onion scattered on top. Costs cents, adds perceived value.

## 9. RM5 HAWKER VS RM500 RESTAURANT: SAME FOOD, DIFFERENT PHOTO

### The Transformation Checklist

**Same char kway teow. Two completely different photos. Here's why:**

#### The RM5 Hawker Photo (Typical Problems)
- Shot under fluorescent/mixed lighting (green-yellow cast)
- Served in styrofoam/melamine plate with mismatched utensils
- Shot from directly above with phone flash
- Cluttered background (other dishes, tissue boxes, sauce bottles)
- No garnish beyond what comes with the dish
- Photo taken quickly, no thought to composition
- Colors are muddy, flat, lifeless
- Makes you think "it's food"

#### The RM500 Restaurant Photo (What We're Aiming For)
- Soft side-light, warm color temperature, natural shadows
- Plated on textured dark ceramic or wooden board
- Shot at 45-degree angle with slight depth of field blur
- Clean surface with maybe one linen napkin and chopsticks
- Fresh garnish: crispy shallots placed deliberately, coriander, lime wedge
- Intentional negative space, rule-of-thirds composition
- Rich warm tones, high clarity on textures, gentle contrast
- Makes you think "I NEED to eat this right now"

#### What Changed? NOT The Food.
1. Light direction (side vs overhead)
2. Color temperature (warm vs fluorescent)
3. Serving vessel (premium ceramic vs plastic)
4. Background (clean surface vs clutter)
5. Garnish (intentional vs none)
6. Composition (deliberate vs random)
7. Post-processing (warm color grade, clarity, contrast)

### For GrabFood Enhancement: The Realistic Scope

We can fix with AI/post-processing:
- Color temperature (warm it up)
- Color grading (rich, warm, appetizing)
- Contrast and clarity (bring out texture)
- Background cleanup/simplification
- Brightness and exposure correction
- Slight shadow direction simulation
- Saturation boost on food colors (reds, oranges, yellows)

We CANNOT fix with AI/post-processing:
- The plate/serving vessel (it's baked into the photo)
- The angle (fundamental perspective is fixed)
- The plating/styling (food is already arranged)
- Missing garnish (AI-generated garnish looks fake)
- The fundamental lighting setup (we can adjust, not recreate)

## 11. MALAYSIAN/SEA FOOD PHOTOGRAPHY VS WESTERN

### Key Differences

| Aspect | Western Standard | Malaysian/SEA Reality |
|--------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Lighting | Studio or controlled natural | Often fluorescent/mixed |
| Surfaces | Styled surfaces, backdrops | Often the actual restaurant table |
| Plating | Minimalist, designed | Abundant, generous portions |
| Color grade | Consistent, branded | Often unedited or over-filtered |
| Props | Curated, intentional | Whatever is on the table |
| Composition | Rule of thirds, negative space | Centered, fill the frame |
| Post-processing | Subtle, professional | Either none or heavy filters |

### Where SEA Food Photography EXCELS
- **Abundance aesthetics** — SEA food photos that show GENEROUS portions with multiple dishes (the "spread" shot) perform extremely well. This is culturally authentic.
- **Color vibrancy** — Malaysian/Thai/Indonesian food is naturally COLORFUL. When properly photographed, it's more visually striking than most Western cuisine.
- **Steam/smoke** — Hot food with visible steam is very common in SEA and highly appetizing.
- **Communal presentation** — Multiple dishes shared around a table is the SEA norm and creates amazing "foodscape" photos.

### Where SEA Food Photography STRUGGLES
- **Lighting infrastructure** — Most hawker stalls/kopitiams have terrible lighting
- **Serving vessel quality** — Melamine/styrofoam vs ceramic/stoneware
- **Background control** — Busy environments, cluttered tables
- **Color consistency** — Mixed lighting creates muddy, inconsistent colors

### For GrabFood Enhancement: The Opportunity
Malaysian food is BEAUTIFUL. The ingredients are colorful, the flavors are complex, and the variety is stunning. The gap is almost entirely in PRESENTATION and PHOTOGRAPHY TECHNIQUE, not in the food itself. Enhancement should:
- Fix lighting/color issues (warm up, correct casts)
- Boost the natural vibrancy of the food
- Clean up backgrounds where possible
- Add clarity and texture detail
- Make the food look as good as it TASTES

## 12. DELIVERY APP PHOTO OPTIMIZATION

### The Data

- **High-quality food photos increase orders by 35%** on delivery apps (Snappr research)
- **25-30% conversion rate boost** when switching from text-only to images (Grubhub/DoorDash data)
- **15% increase in delivery orders** attributed to better visual content (Claid.ai research)

### Delivery App Photo Requirements

#### GrabFood / FoodPanda Malaysia Specs
- **Resolution**: At least 4000 x 2925 pixels (foodpanda requirement)
- **View**: Top or front view (overhead or 45-degree)
- **No watermarks, no branding, no close-ups, no people**
- **File names** should match dish names
- **Single dish focus** — one menu item per photo

#### What Converts at Thumbnail Size

Delivery app photos appear at **~80-200px thumbnails** on phone screens. At this size:
- **Busy backgrounds = visual noise** (use clean, simple backgrounds)
- **Tight crop on the dish** — fill the frame
- **High contrast** — food must POP against background
- **Vibrant, saturated colors** — muted tones disappear at small sizes
- **Clean white background** = maximum contrast, zero distraction
- **Alternatively, bold single-color background** matched to brand = scroll stopper

### The Optimal Delivery App Photo

```
COMPOSITION:  Single dish, centered, filling 70-80% of frame
ANGLE:        45-degree (shows depth) or slight overhead
BACKGROUND:   Clean white, soft grey, or dark solid
LIGHTING:     Warm, directional, texture-revealing
COLOR:        Saturated, warm, high contrast
GARNISH:      Fresh, relevant, minimal (1-2 elements)
VESSEL:       Clean-rimmed plate/bowl (ideally ceramic, not plastic)
```

## 14. PROFESSIONAL RETOUCHING TECHNIQUES

### Core Techniques (What Makes Photos Look Pro)

#### Color & Light
- **Curves/Levels adjustment** — Control tonal range precisely
- **Selective color correction** — Boost specific food colors without affecting the whole image
- **White balance correction** — Remove unwanted color casts
- **Gradient filters** — Even out uneven lighting
- **Color grading** — Apply consistent mood (warm, golden, rich)

#### Texture & Detail
- **Frequency separation** — Enhance texture while keeping colors smooth
- **Dodge and burn** — Sculpt light and shadow to add dimension
- **Clarity adjustments** — Make textures pop (crispy edges, sauce sheen, grain of rice)
- **Sharpening** — Selective sharpening on food details, not backgrounds

#### Cleanup & Composition
- **Healing brush/Clone stamp** — Remove imperfections, crumbs in wrong places, plate chips
- **Background replacement/cleanup** — Simplify or smooth messy backgrounds
- **Compositing** — Combine multiple shots for the perfect single image (commercial standard)
- **Shadow/reflection creation** — Add realistic shadows for depth

#### The Natural-Looking Secret
- **Flow at 10%**, not 100% — subtle, gradual adjustments
- **Multiple subtle passes** rather than one heavy edit
- **Preserve natural highlights and shadows** — don't flatten
- **Keep some imperfection** — too perfect = fake

## 15. NANO PROMPT DNA: REPLICATING THE AESTHETIC

### How to Translate This Research Into AI Enhancement Prompts

Based on all research above, here are the EXACT visual qualities to target when enhancing GrabFood photos:

### The "Premium Food Photo" DNA Blocks

#### BLOCK 1: Lighting
```
Warm directional side-light from left, soft diffused quality,
gentle shadows on right side, subtle golden highlights on food surface,
no harsh flash, no flat overhead light, light temperature 5500-6000K warm
```

#### BLOCK 2: Color
```
Warm color grade, slightly boosted reds/oranges/yellows,

NO green fluorescent cast, NO cool/blue tones on food
```

#### BLOCK 3: Texture
```
High clarity on food surfaces, visible texture detail,
crispy edges sharp, sauce sheen glowing, grain of rice distinct,
soft background, food is tactile and dimensional
```

#### BLOCK 4: Background
```
Clean, uncluttered, simple surface,
warm-toned (cream, warm grey, natural wood, or dark slate),
subtle texture (not perfectly smooth), out of focus if environmental,
no distracting elements, no clutter
```

#### BLOCK 5: Composition
```
Single dish as hero, 70-80% frame fill,
slight negative space for breathing room,
food centered or rule-of-thirds positioned,
no cut-off elements at edges
```

#### BLOCK 6: Mood
```
Appetizing, warm, inviting, fresh, generous,
"I want to eat this right now" feeling,
real and honest, not over-processed,
premium but approachable
```

### Enhancement Priority Matrix

For a typical GrabFood merchant phone photo, fix in this order:

1. **COLOR TEMPERATURE** — Warm it up. Remove green/cool casts. (BIGGEST IMPACT)
2. **CONTRAST + CLARITY** — Add dimension. Make textures pop. (SECOND BIGGEST)
3. **SATURATION** — Boost food colors selectively. (MAKES IT POP)
4. **EXPOSURE** — Balance bright/dark areas. (FOUNDATIONAL)
5. **BACKGROUND** — Simplify/clean if possible. (NICE TO HAVE)
6. **SHARPNESS** — Selective on food details. (FINISHING TOUCH)
