## 2. KEY SCIENCE: INGREDIENTS WITH CLINICAL EVIDENCE FOR VERTIGO

### Tier 1: STRONG Evidence

#### Ginkgo Biloba Extract (EGb 761)
- **Mechanism:** Improves blood flow to inner ear and brain; neuroprotective for vestibular neurons
- **Key Study:** RCT with 160 patients, 240mg/day x 12 weeks: 79% rated "very much improved" or "much improved"
- **Effective for:** Vertebrobasilar insufficiency vertigo, cervical vertigo, non-specific vertigo
- **NOT effective for:** BPPV (repositioning maneuvers needed), Meniere's disease
- **Optimal dose:** 120-240mg/day of standardized extract (24% flavonoids, 6% terpene lactones)
- **Standardization required:** Must be 24% ginkgo flavone glycosides + 6% terpene lactones

#### Vitamin D3
- **Mechanism:** Supports calcium metabolism in otoliths (inner ear crystals); deficiency linked to BPPV
- **Key Study:** Published in Neurology journal — Vitamin D + calcium supplementation reduced BPPV recurrence by 59% (RR = 0.41, 95% CI = 0.26-0.65)
- **Dosing protocol:** 400-1000 IU daily (studies used 400 IU 2x/day or 7000 IU/week)
- **Target population:** Especially effective when serum vitamin D < 20 ng/mL (common in elderly)
- **Evidence class:** Class III evidence for BPPV recurrence prevention

### Tier 2: MODERATE Evidence

#### Magnesium
- **Mechanism:** Supports nerve transmission and muscle relaxation; deficiency causes neuromuscular irritability and migraine-associated vertigo
- **Evidence:** Magnesium deficiency linked to vestibular migraine; supplementation reduces migraine-associated vertigo frequency
- **Optimal dose:** 300-400mg daily (citrate or glycinate forms for absorption)
- **Key benefit:** Addresses migraine-associated vertigo (a major subset of chronic dizziness)

#### B Vitamins (B1, B6, B12)
- **Mechanism:** Neurotropic vitamins — essential coenzymes for nervous system maintenance
- **B12 specifically:** Deficiency causes vestibular dysfunction; common in elderly (reduced absorption with age)
- **B6:** Plays role in neurotransmitter synthesis affecting vestibular processing
- **Evidence:** Multiple observational studies linking B vitamin deficiency to vestibular disorders
- **Optimal doses:** B1 (10-25mg), B6 (10-25mg), B12 (500-1000mcg as methylcobalamin)

#### Vinpocetine
- **Mechanism:** Dilates cerebral blood vessels; sodium channel blocker; improves inner ear blood flow
- **Key Study:** 44 patients with vertigo, 15mg/day x 4 weeks: vertigo improved in 100% of patients, nausea reduced in 90%
- **Limitation:** Short half-life (2-4 hours), needs multiple daily doses; limited double-blind studies
- **Regulatory note:** FDA has raised concerns about vinpocetine safety; may be classified as drug in some markets
- **Optimal dose:** 5-10mg, 2-3x daily with food

#### Ginger Root
- **Mechanism:** Anti-nausea (5-HT3 antagonist); anti-inflammatory; reduces vestibular-triggered nausea
- **Key Study:** Double-blind crossover trial: 1g ginger root reduced caloric vestibular stimulation-induced vertigo significantly vs placebo
- **Seasickness trial:** 1g ginger: 72% protection index for vomiting in naval cadets in heavy seas
- **Optimal dose:** 250-500mg standardized extract (or 1g root powder)
- **Key benefit:** Addresses the NAUSEA component (what patients hate most about vertigo episodes)

### Tier 3: EMERGING/SUPPORTIVE Evidence

#### CoQ10
- **Mechanism:** Mitochondrial support for vestibular neurons; antioxidant protection
- **Evidence:** Limited direct vertigo studies; theoretical basis from neuroprotective properties
- **Use case:** Best as supporting ingredient, not primary

#### Zinc
- **Mechanism:** Essential for neural function; some evidence for inner ear health
- **Evidence:** Observational studies linking zinc deficiency to vestibular dysfunction
